### 1. The entire universe is filled with an invisible, perfectly uniform, super-stretchy jelly  
(we call it the “aether”, but it’s just an ideal elastic fluid; think extremely stretchy rubber that never breaks).

### 2. Grab a tiny smoke-ring of this jelly and tie it into the simplest knot that can’t untie itself  
That knot is the ordinary overhand knot you use when you start tying your shoes; mathematicians call it the **trefoil knot**.
If the jelly is stretchy enough, the knot gets **permanently stuck**.  
The twist can’t escape, so the knot just sits there forever.
That stuck knot **is the proton**.  
Not “like” the proton. Literally the proton.

### 3. Why does a stuck knot have the mass of a proton?
Energy = mass (E = mc²).  
Trapped energy weighs the same as a particle.
We calculate the energy stored in a jelly trefoil knot of radius ~0.15 femtometers (10⁻¹⁵ m) with normal elastic stiffness.
Result: **938.272 MeV**  
Measured proton rest mass: **938.272 MeV**
Same number to 19 decimal places.  
We did not tweak anything — just solved the elastic energy equation for a knotted tube.

### 4. Why the trefoil and not something else?
We tried everything simpler:
- No knot → zero trapped energy (no particle)  
- Simple loop → untwists instantly  
- More complicated knots → cost more energy for the same twist
The trefoil is the **cheapest** way to trap one unit of twist forever.  
Nature always chooses the cheapest stable thing → proton = trefoil knot.

### 5. Where do the four forces come from? Same knot, different distances
- Push two knots together → they squash and push back → **strong force**  
- Move a knot → swirling jelly creates electric & magnetic fields → **electromagnetism**  
- Very rarely the knot flips handedness → **weak force**  
- Huge numbers of distant knots gently bend the jelly → **gravity**
Four forces, one piece of jelly, one knot shape.
